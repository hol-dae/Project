What Is Climate Resilience Tech Adoption?

As climate change accelerates, farmers around the world are adopting new technologies to protect their yields and livelihoods. From soil sensors to autonomous tractors, these innovations help mitigate the effects of extreme weather, rising temperatures, and shifting rainfall patterns. By leveraging data-driven insights, farmers can make informed decisions about planting, irrigation, and crop protection. Technologies such as AI-powered forecasting, smart irrigation systems, and climate-resilient crop varieties not only boost productivity but also enhance sustainability and long-term farm viability.

Drought Resilience

Adopting drought-tolerant crops and precision irrigation systems to conserve water and maximize yield during dry spells.

Technologies include drip irrigation, mulching, and drought-tolerant maize and wheat strains developed by CIMMYT and IRRI.

  • Drip Irrigation: Delivers water directly to plant roots, reducing evaporation and water waste.
  • Mulching: Conserves soil moisture and suppresses weeds, improving drought resilience.
  • Drought-Tolerant Crops: Varieties such as DT maize and wheat can maintain yields under water stress.
  • Remote Sensing: Satellite and sensor data help farmers monitor soil moisture and schedule irrigation efficiently.
  • Case Example: In Kenya, adoption of drip irrigation and DT maize increased yields by up to 40% during dry years.

Flood Tolerance

Using flood-tolerant rice varieties and drainage monitoring tools to recover quickly after heavy rains or monsoon floods.

Farmers in Bangladesh and India now use submersion-tolerant rice ("Scuba Rice") which can survive underwater for up to two weeks without damage.

  • Scuba Rice (Swarna-Sub1): Developed by IRRI, this rice variety can withstand prolonged flooding, helping farmers recover quickly after monsoon floods.
  • Drainage Monitoring: Sensors and remote monitoring tools help manage water levels and prevent crop loss in flood-prone areas.
  • Community Action: Farmer cooperatives in Bangladesh coordinate planting and drainage schedules to maximize survival rates.
  • Case Example: In 2022, over 5 million Bangladeshi farmers adopted flood-tolerant rice, reducing post-flood yield losses by up to 80%.

New crop strains and shade-integrated farming methods are helping farmers cope with rising temperatures and heatwaves.

Researchers at ICAR and ICRISAT have introduced heat-resistant sorghum and millets that mature faster before peak summer heat hits.

Salt Tolerance

Cultivating crops in saline soils using salt-tolerant barley and rice strains.

Salt-tolerant crops like barley and rice are now grown successfully in coastal regions affected by seawater intrusion and overuse of fertilizers.

  • Salt-Tolerant Rice (e.g., Pokkali, IRRI lines): Developed for deltas and coastal areas, these varieties can thrive in saline soils where traditional crops fail.
  • Barley and Quinoa: Naturally salt-tolerant, these crops are increasingly adopted in arid and semi-arid regions facing soil salinization.
  • Soil Amendments: Use of gypsum and organic matter helps reclaim saline soils and improve crop establishment.

AI Forecasting

Machine learning models analyze historical and real-time data to predict optimal planting and harvesting times.

AI-powered platforms like Microsoft FarmBeats and AgNext offer predictive analytics for weather, pest control, and resource planning.

  • Weather Prediction: Machine learning models process satellite and sensor data to forecast rainfall, drought, and extreme events, helping farmers plan ahead.
  • Pest & Disease Alerts: AI analyzes crop images and environmental data to detect early signs of pest outbreaks or disease, enabling timely interventions.
  • Yield Optimization: Data-driven recommendations for planting, fertilization, and irrigation schedules maximize productivity and reduce waste.
  • Case Example: In India, AI-based advisories from Microsoft FarmBeats helped farmers increase chickpea yields by 30% through optimized sowing dates and input use.

Regenerative Agriculture

Practices such as no-till farming, cover cropping, and biochar application improve soil health and carbon capture.

Regenerative practices restore soil fertility, increase biodiversity, and reduce dependency on chemical inputs â€” making farms more resilient to long-term climate shifts.

  • No-Till Farming: Reduces soil erosion and preserves soil structure by minimizing disturbance, leading to improved water retention and carbon sequestration.
  • Cover Cropping: Planting cover crops like clover or vetch between main crops protects soil from erosion, fixes nitrogen, and suppresses weeds naturally.
  • Biochar Application: Adding biochar (charcoal from plant matter) to soil enhances nutrient retention, boosts microbial activity, and locks carbon in the ground for decades.
  • Composting & Organic Amendments: Using compost and manure increases organic matter, improves soil health, and reduces reliance on synthetic fertilizers.
  • Agroforestry: Integrating trees and shrubs into cropland provides shade, windbreaks, and habitat for beneficial insects and birds.
  • Case Example: In Brazil, regenerative agriculture on degraded pastureland increased soil organic carbon by 20% and doubled maize yields within three years.

Solar-Powered Water Pumps

Cost-effective, renewable energy solution for smallholder farmers facing unreliable electricity and water shortages.

Used widely in India and East Africa, solar pumps provide sustainable access to water without reliance on diesel or electric grids.

  • Renewable Energy: Solar panels power water pumps, reducing fuel costs and greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Low Maintenance: Fewer moving parts mean less frequent repairs compared to diesel pumps.
  • Remote Operation: Some systems can be monitored and controlled via mobile apps for convenience.
  • Scalable Solutions: Available for both smallholder and large-scale farms, adaptable to different water needs.
  • Case Example: In Kenya, adoption of solar pumps by smallholder farmers increased irrigation coverage by 50% and reduced energy costs by up to 80%.

Soil Health Monitoring

IoT-enabled sensors track moisture, pH, and nutrient levels to guide better decisions and prevent losses.

Tools like Teralytic and Arable Meters help farmers adjust irrigation and fertilizer use dynamically based on sensor readings.

  • Soil Moisture Sensors: Provide real-time data to prevent over- or under-watering, improving crop health and saving water.
  • pH and Nutrient Sensors: Detect soil acidity and nutrient deficiencies, enabling targeted fertilizer application and reducing waste.
  • Wireless Connectivity: Many sensors transmit data to mobile apps or dashboards, allowing remote monitoring and alerts.
  • Case Example: In California, almond growers using soil sensors reduced water use by 20% while maintaining yields.

Drone-Based Field Monitoring

High-resolution drone imaging helps detect early signs of stress in crops and plan interventions accordingly.

Drone sprayers and multispectral cameras are used in Brazil, Kenya, and the Philippines to map farmland and identify stressed areas before they fail.

  • Multispectral Imaging: Drones capture images in multiple wavelengths to detect crop stress, disease, or nutrient deficiencies early.
  • Precision Spraying: Drone sprayers apply fertilizers or pesticides only where needed, reducing chemical use and costs.
  • Large Area Coverage: Drones can survey hundreds of hectares in a single flight, providing rapid, up-to-date field data.
  • Automated Analysis: AI-powered software processes drone data to generate actionable maps for irrigation, fertilization, or replanting.
  • Case Example: In China, drone monitoring reduced rice crop losses by 15% through early pest detection and targeted spraying.

Case Studies

Zambia - Drought-Tolerant Maize

Over 10,000 Zambian farmers adopted drought-tolerant maize, increasing average yield by 25% during dry seasons.

Supported by FAO and local extension services, this project helped reduce food insecurity in Southern Zambia.

  • Background: Southern Zambia is highly vulnerable to droughts, which have historically led to crop failures and food shortages. Traditional maize varieties struggled to survive prolonged dry spells, threatening farmer livelihoods.
  • Technology Introduced: Drought-tolerant maize hybrids, developed through partnerships with CIMMYT and local research institutes, were distributed to over 10,000 smallholder farmers. These varieties can maintain yields with less rainfall and mature earlier than conventional types.
  • Implementation: The project included farmer field schools, demonstration plots, and training on climate-smart practices such as conservation tillage, mulching, and efficient fertilizer use. Extension agents provided ongoing support and weather advisories.
  • Results:
    • Average maize yields increased by 25% during drought years compared to previous seasons.
    • Household food security improved, with more families able to store surplus grain for the dry season.
    • Adoption of conservation agriculture practices led to better soil moisture retention and reduced erosion.
    • Women farmers, who make up a large share of participants, reported greater resilience and income stability.
  • Challenges: Initial skepticism about new seed varieties and limited access to credit for inputs slowed early adoption. Ongoing education and demonstration of results helped build trust.
  • Lessons Learned: Combining improved seed with farmer training and community support networks is critical for successful technology adoption. Local adaptation and feedback from farmers ensured the varieties met real-world needs.
  • Future Plans: The program is expanding to include drought-tolerant legumes and digital weather advisory services, aiming to reach 50,000 farmers by 2027.
  • Key Partners: FAO, Zambia Agricultural Research Institute, CIMMYT, local NGOs, and farmer cooperatives.

Bangladesh - Flood Tolerant Rice

Submersion-tolerant rice varieties helped Bangladeshi farmers recover faster after seasonal flooding.

The Scuba Rice program expanded into Nepal and Myanmar, saving harvests during flash floods in delta regions.

  • Background: Bangladesh is one of the most flood-prone countries in the world, with millions of hectares of farmland submerged annually during the monsoon season. Traditional rice varieties often fail after just a few days underwater, leading to widespread crop losses and food insecurity.
  • Technology Introduced: Submersion-tolerant rice varieties, such as Swarna-Sub1 (commonly called "Scuba Rice"), were developed by the International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) using marker-assisted breeding. These varieties can survive complete submergence for up to two weeks, allowing crops to recover after floodwaters recede.
  • Implementation: The government of Bangladesh, in partnership with IRRI and local agricultural extension services, distributed Scuba Rice seeds to over 5 million farmers in flood-prone districts. Training sessions and demonstration plots were established to show the benefits and proper cultivation techniques for the new varieties.
  • Results:
    • Farmers adopting Scuba Rice experienced up to 80% reduction in yield losses after floods compared to traditional rice.
    • Average rice yields increased by 1-2 tons per hectare in affected areas, improving food security and household incomes.
    • Recovery time after floods was shortened, allowing some farmers to plant a second crop in the same season.
    • Women farmers, who are often responsible for transplanting and harvesting rice, reported greater resilience and more stable livelihoods.
  • Challenges: Initial skepticism about the new varieties, limited access to quality seed, and the need for farmer training slowed early adoption. Some farmers also faced difficulties integrating Scuba Rice into existing cropping systems or accessing markets for surplus production.
  • Lessons Learned: Success depended on strong partnerships between research institutions, government agencies, and local communities. Demonstration plots and farmer-to-farmer knowledge sharing were critical for building trust and encouraging widespread adoption. Timely seed distribution and extension support before the monsoon season were also essential.
  • Expansion: The success of Scuba Rice in Bangladesh led to its introduction in other flood-prone regions, including Nepal, India, and Myanmar. Ongoing research is developing new submergence-tolerant varieties with improved yield, grain quality, and disease resistance.
  • Key Partners: International Rice Research Institute (IRRI), Bangladesh Rice Research Institute (BRRI), Ministry of Agriculture, local NGOs, and farmer cooperatives.
